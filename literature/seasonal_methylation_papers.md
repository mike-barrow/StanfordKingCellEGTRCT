## Paper 1: Ricceri et al. (2014) — Most Directly Relevant

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **PMID** | 25210735 |
| **DOI** | 10.1371/journal.pone.0106846 |
| **Title** | Seasonality modifies methylation profiles in healthy people |
| **Authors** | Ricceri F, Trevisan M, Fiano V, Grasso C, Fasanelli F, Scoccianti C, De Marco L, Tos AG, Vineis P, Sacerdote C |
| **Journal** | PLoS ONE |
| **Year** | 2014 |
| **Tissue** | Peripheral blood (whole blood) |
| **N** | 88 healthy male heavy smokers (Italian) |

**Key finding:** DNA methylation was measured at LINE-1 (global methylation proxy), RASSF1A, and MGMT genes by pyrosequencing. Methylation was significantly higher in spring/summer compared to autumn/winter:
- LINE-1: 68.26% (spring/summer) vs 67.43% (autumn/winter) — significantly hypomethylated in autumn/winter (p=0.04–0.05)
- RASSF1A: 2.35% vs 2.17%
- MGMT: 9.52% vs 8.60%

**Relevance to EGT RCT:** This paper directly demonstrates that DNA methylation levels in blood fluctuate seasonally — methylation is lower in winter and higher in summer. A 12-month study covers one full annual cycle, thereby controlling for this seasonal drift that could confound results in a shorter study.

## Paper 2: Waterland et al. (2010) — Foundational Seasonal Epigenetic Evidence

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **PMID** | 21203497 |
| **DOI** | 10.1371/journal.pgen.1001252 |
| **Title** | Season of conception in rural Gambia affects DNA methylation at putative human metastable epialleles |
| **Authors** | Waterland RA, Kellermayer R, Laritsky E, Rayco-Solon P, Harris RA, Travisano M, Zhang W, Torskaya MS, Zhang J, Shen L, Manary MJ, Prentice AM |
| **Journal** | PLoS Genetics |
| **Year** | 2010 |
| **Tissue** | Peripheral blood, hair follicles, and autopsy tissues (multiple germ layers) |

**Key finding:** Conception during the nutritionally challenged rainy season (when food is scarce) led to elevated DNA methylation at metastable epialleles (MEs) compared to the dry season. These MEs showed systemic interindividual variation correlating across all three germ layers, meaning blood DNA methylation at these loci reflects methylation in internal organs. This was the first demonstration of a permanent, systemic effect of periconceptional seasonal environment on the human epigenome.

**Relevance to EGT RCT:** While focused on periconceptional programming, this paper established that seasonal nutritional/environmental variation causes durable methylation differences. It reinforces the rationale that seasonal confounders affect the methylome and must be controlled by a complete annual cycle.

## Summary for Protocol Argument

Both papers support the claim that **seasonal variation in DNA methylation is a real, measurable phenomenon in humans.** Specifically:

1. **Ricceri et al.** shows that blood DNA methylation levels at specific loci (LINE-1, RASSF1A, MGMT) oscillate with the seasons — lower in autumn/winter, higher in spring/summer. This is the cleanest evidence for seasonal methylation drift in human blood.

2. **Waterland et al.** shows that the season of periconceptional exposure leaves a lasting mark on the epigenome, establishing that seasonal environmental fluctuations (nutrition, sunlight, etc.) are potent modulators of DNA methylation.

### For the EGT RCT protocol:
> A 12-month study duration ensures that every subject passes through all four seasons, fully controlling for the seasonal methylation drift documented by Ricceri et al. (2014). Shorter studies (e.g., 3–6 months) could confound intervention effects with the natural seasonal oscillation of ~1% in LINE-1 methylation and similar fluctuations at gene-specific loci, potentially masking or exaggerating treatment effects.
