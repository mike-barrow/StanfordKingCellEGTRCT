# GLP-1 Receptor Agonists, Glycemic Control, and Epigenetic Aging

Prepared for the Michael Snyder call — explains why the semaglutide/Corley 2025
precedent is relevant to his interest in glycemic control and multiomics.

## What Is Semaglutide (Ozempic / Wegovy)?

- A **GLP-1 receptor agonist** — mimics the incretin hormone glucagon-like peptide-1
- Primary clinical use: **type 2 diabetes** (glycemic control) and **obesity** (weight loss)
- Marketed as Ozempic (diabetes), Wegovy (weight loss), Rybelsus (oral)
- Mechanism: stimulates insulin secretion, suppresses glucagon, slows gastric emptying,
  reduces appetite

## Why It Matters for Epigenetic Aging

The **Corley et al. 2025 trial** (now published in Nature Communications 2026) tested
semaglutide in people with HIV and lipohypertrophy (abnormal fat distribution). Key facts:

- **32-week, double-blind, placebo-controlled** — 84 participants (45 semaglutide, 39 placebo)
- **No diabetes** — baseline HbA1c was **5.5%** (normal). The trial excluded diabetics.
- **Results across all clocks:**
  - GrimAge2: -2.3 years (p=0.009)
  - PhenoAge: -4.9 years (p=0.004)
  - DunedinPACE: -0.09 units (p=0.01, ~9% slower aging pace)
  - PCGrimAge: -3.1 years (p=0.007)

### The Glycemic Connection

Even though the trial excluded diabetics, semaglutide's mechanism is fundamentally
**metabolic** — it improves insulin sensitivity, reduces visceral adiposity, and lowers
inflammation (hsCRP, sCD163 were covariates in the analysis). The clocks that responded
most strongly (PhenoAge, GrimAge2) include glucose and metabolic biomarkers in their
training sets.

**Takeaway for Snyder:** A drug that works through metabolic/glycemic pathways slowed
epigenetic aging across multiple clocks in 8 months. This establishes that the
metabolic state → epigenetic aging axis is real and intervention-responsive.
Our EGT-RCT tests whether a nutraceutical acting through SIRT6 (a master regulator
of both glucose metabolism and chromatin) produces a similar signal through a
different entry point.

## Why This Isn't "EGT as Glucose-Lowering Drug"

- EGT does NOT lower glucose directly (no clinical data suggests this)
- D'Onofrio 2016 shows EGT upregulates SIRT1/SIRT6 **under high-glucose conditions**,
  preventing endothelial senescence — a protective effect, not a glucose-lowering effect
- SIRT6 is the mechanistic bridge: it's a **chromatin regulator** (deacetylates H3K9, H3K56)
  AND a **metabolic sensor** (regulates glucose homeostasis via HIF1a and G6PC)
- The Corley precedent shows that metabolic interventions can slow clocks — EGT's
  SIRT6 mechanism is a different, chromatin-based path to the same destination
