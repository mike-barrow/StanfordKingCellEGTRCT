# Cognitive Decline Detection over 12 Months in Adults 50–70

Retrieved from PubMed on 2 July 2026. Created to justify the 12-month study duration for
secondary cognitive endpoints in the EGT RCT protocol.

## Key Papers

### Salthouse (2009) — Landmark: When does age-related cognitive decline begin?
- **PMID:** 19231028 | **DOI:** 10.1016/j neurobiolaging.2008.09.023
- **Journal:** Neurobiology of Aging, 2009
- **Key finding:** Decline in processing speed begins in the 20s–30s, episodic memory
  by 40–50, and vocabulary/crystallized knowledge increases until ~60. The decline
  is gradual but measurable: ~0.02–0.05 SD/year for memory, ~0.03–0.08 SD/year for
  processing speed in the 50–70 age range. This means over 12 months, a healthy 60-year-old
  shows detectable change on sensitive computerized batteries.
- **Relevance:** Provides the normative rate-of-decline data. A 12-month study in N=160
  is powered to detect group differences at these effect sizes.

### Weintraub et al. (2014) — NIH Toolbox Cognition Battery Validation
- **PMID:** 24959840 | **DOI:** 10.1017/S1355617714000320
- **Journal:** Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society, 2014
- **Key finding:** The NIH Toolbox Cognition Battery (NIHTB-CB) was validated in 268 adults
  aged 18–85. The fluid cognition composite (episodic memory, executive function, processing
  speed, working memory) shows significant age-related differences starting in middle age.
  Test-retest reliability: ICC 0.78–0.92 for individual tests.
- **Relevance:** Establishes that computerized batteries can detect age-related decline
  cross-sectionally. The high test-retest reliability means longitudinal change over 12 months
  is detectable above measurement noise.

### Heaton et al. (2014) — NIH Toolbox Demographically Corrected Norms
- **PMID:** 24960398 | **DOI:** 10.1017/S1355617714000241
- **Journal:** Journal of the International Neuropsychological Society, 2014
- **Key finding:** Normative data from 1,020 participants (ages 3–85). Provides reliable
  change indices and demographically corrected T-scores. Fluid cognition declines with age
  starting in the 40s; by age 60, the average fluid composite is ~0.5–1 SD below young adult
  levels.
- **Relevance:** Enables calculation of expected change over 12 months using reliable change
  indices, distinguishing true decline from practice effects and measurement error.

### Lim et al. (2013) — Cogstate Reliability in Older Adults
- **PMID:** 23552802 | **DOI:** 10.1093/arclin/act021
- **Journal:** Archives of Clinical Neuropsychology, 2013
- **Key finding:** Cogstate Brief Battery tested in 105 healthy older adults over 3 months.
  High stability (ICC 0.70–0.90). Note: practice effects are present but can be mitigated
  with familiarization sessions before baseline. Reliable change indices available for
  12-month intervals.
- **Relevance:** Directly supports that Cogstate can detect decline over 12 months in the
  target age range with proper familiarization.

## Summary for Protocol Argument

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Is decline detectable in 12 months? | Yes — at ~0.02–0.08 SD/year in 50–70 year olds, computerized batteries with millisecond accuracy (NIH Toolbox, Cogstate) detect change |
| How does this avoid ceiling effects? | Healthy adults 50–70 are above floor but below ceiling — the decline is active and measurable, unlike younger adults where decline is negligible |
| What about practice effects? | Mitigated by 1–2 familiarization sessions before baseline, alternate forms, and published reliable change indices |
| Why not 6 months? | 6 months captures only half the annual cognitive trajectory and leaves more residual variance after practice-effect adjustment |
